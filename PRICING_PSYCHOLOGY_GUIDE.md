## The 5 Psychological Pricing Principles (for Technical Services)

### 1. Charm Pricing (Why A$497, Not A$500)

The left-digit effect: A$497 is perceived as "four hundred something" while A$500 is "five hundred." The 2% difference in price creates a ~10% difference in perceived value.

| Bad | Better | Why |
|---|---|---|
| A$500 | **A$497** | "Four hundred and..." vs "Five hundred" |
| A$1,500 | **A$1,497** | Same effect at scale |
| A$200 | **A$197** | Reads as under A$200 |
| A$100/hr | **A$97/hr** | Under the psychological century mark |

**Your prices already use this:** A$497, A$1,497, A$2,497. Don't round up.

---

### 2. The Decoy Effect (Why 3 Tiers Always)

Your three tiers aren't really about giving choice — they're about making the middle one look like the obvious value.

| Tier | Price | Psychological Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Basic (AI Audit)** | A$497 | **Anchor low.** Makes the other tiers look "only X more." |
| **Standard (AI Build)** | A$1,497 | **The target.** This is what you want them to buy. Priced at 3× Basic but delivers 10× value. |
| **Premium (AI Partner)** | A$2,497/mo | **Anchor high.** Makes Standard look affordable by comparison. Also captures the high-budget buyer. |

**Why this works:** Without the Premium tier, Standard looks expensive. With it, Standard is "the smart choice — not the cheapest, not the most expensive."

**Rule:** Never drop the Premium tier, even if nobody buys it. It's not there to be sold — it's there to sell the Standard tier.

---

### 3. Anchoring (Set the Frame First)

The first number a prospect hears becomes the anchor for every subsequent number.

**Bad (let the prospect set the anchor):**
> Prospect: "What do you charge?"
> You: "A$1,497."
> Prospect: "Whoa." (They anchored at $200 in their head.)

**Good (you set the anchor):**
> "You mentioned this is costing you roughly A$2,000/month. Our engagement fee is A$1,497 — that's less than 1 month of your current pain, with a payback period of about {X} weeks."

Now A$1,497 is compared to A$2,000/month — it looks cheap.

**Better (use the ROI calculator first):**
> "Before I quote, let's quickly punch your numbers into this calculator. [Open `AUTOMATION_ROI_CALCULATOR.html`]. So your manual work is costing you ~A$93,600/year. Our A$1,497 build saves ~A$74,880 of that."

Now A$1,497 is compared to A$74,880 — it's insignificant.

---

### 4. Bundling (Sell Outcomes, Not Hours)

Never price by the hour. Hourly pricing caps your income and makes clients scrutinise every minute.

**Instead, bundle:**

| Bundle Name | What's In It | Price | Perceived Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| "AI Audit" | 90-min interview + process map + scorecard + roadmap | A$497 | Specific, concrete outcome |
| "AI Build" | Full audit + workflow built + deployed + documented + 30-day warranty | A$1,497 | "Everything included" |
| "AI Partner" | Everything in Build + 3 workflows + monthly tuning + priority support + quarterly strategy | A$2,497/mo | "Done for you, forever" |

**Why bundling works:**
- Clients can't price-shop individual components
- "30-day warranty" has enormous perceived value (costs you almost nothing)
- "Quarterly strategy review" sounds premium (a 30-min call you'd do anyway)

---

### 5. Price Anchoring by Context

| Context | How to Frame |
|---|---|
| **Discovery call** | Compare to cost of inaction: "You're losing A$2k/month. Our fix is A$1,497 once — 3 weeks to break even." |
| **Against agencies** | "They charge A$50k minimum for a 6-week discovery. I deliver working automation in 14 days for A$1,497." |
| **Against Fiverr** | "A$200 gets you a deliverable. A$1,497 gets you an outcome — with warranty, documentation, and actual error handling." |
| **For ROI** | "A$1,497 saves you ~A$75k/year. That's a 50:1 return in year one." |

## When to Raise Prices (And By How Much)

| Signal | Action |
|---|---|
| **You're booked 3+ weeks out** | Raise prices 25-50%. Scarcity is real. |
| **3+ clients accepted your quote without hesitation** | You're underpriced. Raise 20% on next quote. |
| **80%+ of proposals close** | You're below market. Raise 30%+. |
| **No one has flinched at pricing in 5+ calls** | Raise 25%. You should get a "can we scope back?" at least occasionally. |
| **Fiverr gig has 10+ 5★ reviews** | Raise Fiverr price 30%. Reviews justify premium. |
| **You delivered 5+ projects** | Portfolio justifies a rate increase. Raise 25-50%. |
| **You have a unique specialization** (security+automation) | Charge 20-30% above generalist rates. Specialists command premiums. |

**When NOT to raise prices:**
- During an active client project (wait until next engagement)
- When you have <3 projects delivered (build portfolio first)
- When you're desperate for cash (desperation pricing leads to bad decisions)

## How to Raise Prices on Existing Clients

**Grandfather rule:** Existing clients stay at their current rate for 6-12 months after a price increase. They're your referral engines — keep them happy.

**Notification template:**

> **Subject:** AusAI Tech — pricing update for new engagements
>
> Hi {NAME},
>
> Just a heads-up: as of {date}, my rates for new engagements are increasing to A${new price}. This reflects {reason — more experience, growing demand, expanded capabilities}.
>
> **Your current rate is grandfathered** — no changes to our existing arrangement. If you want to lock in an additional project at the current rate before {date}, just let me know.
>
> Best,
> Karma

## Handling Price Objections with Psychology

### "That's expensive"

**Don't defend the price. Reframe against the alternative:**

> "Compared to what? If the alternative is doing nothing and losing A${their cost of inaction}/year, A${your price} is cheap. If the alternative is a Fiverr freelancer at A$200 who delivers with no documentation or warranty, those are different services. I'm happy to help you figure out which one is the right fit."

---

### "Can you do a discount?"

**Never discount the price. Scope back the engagement:**

> "I can't discount the rate — it wouldn't be fair to clients who pay full price. But I can scope the engagement back to fit your budget. If we drop {deliverable}, that brings it to A${lower amount}. Then you can add it later when the budget allows."

This preserves your rate integrity while being helpful.

**One exception:** Portfolio-building discount for your first 2-3 clients only. Frame it as: "I'm offering a foundation-client rate of A${X} (normally A${Y}) in exchange for a testimonial and permission to use the project as a case study." This is a fair trade, not a discount.

---

### "X charges less"

> "Totally fair to compare. A few things to check when you compare: 1) Do they include a warranty? 2) Do they provide documentation and training? 3) Do they handle error cases and edge conditions? 4) What happens on day 31? If all of those match, they might be the better deal. If not, you're comparing different services at different prices."

---

### "We don't have the budget"

> "I understand. Two options: 1) We scope back to something that fits your budget right now — I can deliver the core automation for A${X}. 2) We start with the A$497 AI Audit. That gives you a prioritised roadmap, and you can phase the build across 2-3 months as budget frees up."

## The Pricing Psychology Cheat Sheet

```
🔢 PRICING PSYCHOLOGY RULES:

1. Charm prices: A$497, A$1,497, A$2,497 — never round
2. 3 tiers always: Basic (anchor low) — Standard (target) — Premium (anchor high)
3. Anchor against pain: "Your problem costs A$X/month. Our solution is A$Y."
4. Bundle, never hourly: sell outcomes, not time
5. 50/50 payment: reduces sticker shock, protects you
6. Never discount. Scope back instead.
7. Grandfather existing clients: they're your referral engine
8. Raise when booked out or nobody flinches
9. First 2-3 clients: portfolio discount is OK (testimonial trade)
10. If they don't flinch, you underquoted. If they gasp, scope back.
```
