# Scope Creep Defense Playbook — AusAI Tech

> **Purpose:** Deep psychological and tactical defense against scope creep — beyond the basic Change Order process in `First_Client_Onboarding.md`. This is your fireproof boundary-setting manual.
> **When to use:** The moment a client asks for anything outside the signed SOW. Open this doc before you reply.
> **Prerequisite:** `First_Client_Onboarding.md` (SOW template, basic Change Orders), `PROJECT_MANAGEMENT_SOP.md` (escalation triggers)

---

## The Psychology of Scope Creep

Scope creep isn't malice — it's human nature. Understanding WHY it happens makes it easier to defuse without burning bridges.

| Root Cause | What's Actually Happening | Your Defensive Move |
|---|---|---|
| **"While you're in there..."** | Client sees an opportunity to get more from an already-engaged expert | "I can look at that — here's what it would add to scope." (immediate re-quote) |
| **Fear of incomplete solution** | Client worries the deliverable won't fully solve their problem | Surface this early: "Is there something you're worried the system won't handle?" |
| **Stakeholder pressure** | Client's boss asked for a feature they didn't scope | Offer help framing the ask: "Want me to write a 1-paragraph justification for your boss?" |
| **Shifting requirements** | Their business changed mid-project (legitimate) | Change Order — this one is usually worth doing |
| **Poor initial scoping** | You didn't ask enough questions in intake | Fix your intake process for next time. For now: honest conversation. |
| **Testing the boundary** | Some clients test freelancers to see what's "free" | Firm, polite boundary. If you give in once, it's expected forever. |

## The Escalation Ladder (5 Levels)

Not every scope request needs the same response. Match your intensity to the severity.

| Level | What It Looks Like | Response |
|---|---|---|
| **L1 — Clarification** | "Can you explain how X works?" | Answer for free. This isn't scope creep — it's client education. |
| **L2 — Tiny ask** | "Can we change the Slack channel name?" | Do it for free if <5 min. Say: "Done. Heads up — anything bigger I'll send a Change Order." If they accumulate beyond 2-3 in a week, start tracking and convert to a Change Order. |
| **L3 — Minor addition** | "Can we add one more condition to the workflow?" | Estimate: "That's ~1 hour. I can add it at {rate}. Want a quick Change Order?" |
| **L4 — New feature** | "Can we also auto-generate reports from this?" | Formal Change Order via `Proposal_Template.html`. Must be paid before work starts. |
| **L5 — Project pivot** | "Actually, we need something completely different" | Stop work. Renegotiate SOW or cancel with partial refund. This is a new project. |

**Rule:** Anything that takes >15 minutes or adds a new deliverable = L3 or higher = Change Order.

## The "Nice No" Formula

Saying no without sounding like you're saying no. The formula:

```
1. VALIDATE — Acknowledge their request (makes them feel heard)
2. EXPLAIN — Reference the SOW boundary (depersonalizes it)
3. OFFER — Give them a path forward (keeps you helpful)
4. INVITE — Let them choose (returns agency to them)
```

### Example Scripts

**For a small addition (L3):**

> "Great idea — adding {feature} would make the system even smoother. It's a small addition to scope — estimate about {time} at {rate}. Want me to send a Change Order, or should we keep it on the backlog for Phase 2?" [VALIDATE + EXPLAIN + OFFER + INVITE]

**For a new feature (L4):**

> "I can absolutely build {feature}. It's outside the original SOW scope (Section 3), so it'd be a separate Change Order. I'll quote it — if it works for you, I can get it done by {date}. Want me to send the quote?" [VALIDATE + EXPLAIN + OFFER + INVITE]

**For a project pivot (L5):**

> "That's a different direction than we scoped. No problem — let's treat this as a new engagement. I'd suggest we finish the current build first (you're {X}% done), then scope the new direction fresh. That way you get both, and we don't lose the work already done. Or we can pause this and pivot — your call." [VALIDATE + EXPLAIN + OFFER + INVITE]

**For a request that's bad for the client:**

> "I could do that, but I want to flag something: {reason it's a bad idea}. My recommendation would be {better alternative}. What do you think?" [VALIDATE + WARN + ADVISE + INVITE]

## Boundary-Setting Scripts by Scenario

### "Can you just..." (The Most Dangerous Two Words)

"Just" is the scope creep red flag. Almost nothing is "just" anything.

**Don't say:**
> ❌ "Sure, no problem."

**Say:**
> ✅ "Let me check — 'just' usually means there's complexity I need to look at. Give me 10 minutes to assess and I'll confirm if it's a quick add or needs a Change Order."

This buys time, sets expectation, and establishes that nothing is "just" assumed to be free.

---

### "The last freelancer did this for free"

Common Fiverr/client expectation. Danger zone — they're testing your boundary.

> "Different freelancers scope differently. My engagement includes {what's in scope} and everything else is quoted separately. This keeps it fair — you only pay for what you need, and I don't lose money on scope creep. Does that make sense?"

This frames boundary-setting as *fairness*, not greed.

---

### "My boss just asked for..."

Valid — the client didn't cause this. Help them manage upward.

> "No worries — I get that stakeholder asks come up. Want me to write a 1-paragraph summary of what {feature} would cost and how long it'd add? You can forward it directly to your boss. If they approve, I'll send the formal Change Order."

This makes you their ally against their boss, not their obstacle.

---

### "Can we just add {feature} now and figure out payment later?"

Hard no. Never, ever do this. The payment "later" never comes.

> "I can't start new work without a signed Change Order — it protects both of us. I'll send the quote now. If you approve today, I can start {date}."

No negotiation. No exceptions. This is the one boundary that, if broken, poisons the entire engagement.

---

### The Deadline + Scope Double-Bind

Client wants new feature AND the original deadline unchanged. Mathematically impossible.

> "I can build {feature}, but it adds {X days}. Options: A) I deliver the core scope on {original date} and add {feature} by {new date}. B) We push the whole delivery by {X days}. Which works better for you?"

Make them choose the trade-off. Never absorb the time pressure silently.

## When to Fire a Client

Some scope creep isn't scope creep — it's a bad client. Fire when:

| Signal | Fire If... |
|---|---|
| **3+ unpaid Change Orders requested** | They've asked for 3 things and dodged payment each time |
| **Scope has doubled** since SOW signing | Original scope was a foot in the door; now they want 2× the project for 1× the price |
| **Payment is >14 days late** | And they're still asking for new features |
| **They badmouth you while asking for free work** | "This isn't what I expected, can you also..." — if you're not meeting expectations, adding free work won't fix it |
| **You dread opening their messages** | Trust your gut. Stress cost > remaining revenue |

**Firing script:**

> "Hi {NAME}, I've enjoyed working on {project} but I think we've reached the natural endpoint of this engagement. I'll deliver everything in the current SOW by {date}. After that, I'd recommend {alternative provider / DIY path} for the additional work. No hard feelings — I want to make sure you get what you need, and I don't think I'm the right fit for the expanded scope."

## Prevention: Better Scoping Upfront

The best scope creep defense is a tight SOW. From `First_Client_Onboarding.md`:

- [ ] Section 3 ("NOT IN SCOPE") has at least 5 specific exclusions
- [ ] Section 6 ("ACCEPTANCE") has a measurable criterion, not "looks good"
- [ ] You asked the intake question: "What would make this project feel like a failure?" (catches hidden expectations)
- [ ] You explicitly stated: "Here's what I'm NOT building" during the architecture call

## Track Your Creep

Keep a running tally in your project notes:

```
SCOPE CREEP LOG — {Project Name}
- Date: {date} — Request: {what they asked} — Outcome: Free (L2) / Change Order (L3+) / Declined
```

Review this log at project close. If >30% of your billable hours were scope creep, increase your next quote by 30%. The data doesn't lie.

## Quick-Reference Card (Print This)

```
🛑 BEFORE YOU REPLY TO ANY SCOPE REQUEST:

1. Is it in the SOW? → Yes: do it. No: continue.
2. How long will it take? → <15 min: consider L1/L2. >15 min: L3+.
3. Is it their first ask? → Can be gentler. Is it their 5th? → Firmer.
4. Have they paid on time? → Yes: more flexibility. No: zero flexibility.

FORMULA: Validate + Reference SOW + Offer path + Invite choice
NEVER: Payment later, "just this once," silent absorption
```
